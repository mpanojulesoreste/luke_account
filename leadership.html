Leadership Lessons I Witnessed

Six principles from Jesus's transformative leadership model

Throughout my investigations, I discovered a pattern in how Jesus built his team and prepared them to carry his mission forward. These principles transcend time and culture, offering wisdom for anyone seeking to lead transformation.

What made Jesus's leadership so effective was not just what he taught but how he embodied these principles. His team transformed from ordinary, often conflicted individuals into a cohesive force that would change the world.

1

Begin With a Clear Vision and Mission

"The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he has anointed me to bring good news to the poor. He has sent me to proclaim release to the captives and recovery of sight to the blind, to let the oppressed go free, to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor."

— Jesus in Nazareth (Luke 4:18-19)

Jesus began his public ministry not by selecting his team but by articulating his mission. In the synagogue at Nazareth, he outlined the three pillars of his mission: to bring people to God, to help them live together well, and to teach them to live well individually.

This clarity of purpose attracted the right followers and provided direction for every action. Even when faced with opposition, Jesus stayed focused on this mission, demonstrating that transformative leaders maintain a clear vision even amidst distractions.

I observed how this vision-first approach gave meaning to the disciples' work. They weren't just following a charismatic teacher; they were participating in the fulfillment of ancient promises and the establishment of God's kingdom.

2

Embody the Change You Seek

"I have set you an example, that you also should do as I have done to you."

— Jesus to his disciples

Jesus didn't merely instruct his followers; he modeled the behaviors he expected from them. When teaching about service, he washed their feet. When teaching about compassion, he touched the untouchable. When teaching about forgiveness, he forgave those who wronged him.

This consistency between teaching and action gave Jesus moral authority that transcended his formal position. The disciples frequently looked to him as their example, even when facing challenges after his departure.

Through my research, I found that this principle of embodiment was particularly powerful in Jesus's approach. He didn't ask his followers to go anywhere he hadn't gone himself or to make sacrifices he wasn't willing to make.

3

Select the Right Team Members

"Jesus went out to the mountain to pray; and he spent the night in prayer to God. And when day came, he called his disciples and chose twelve of them, whom he also named apostles."

— Luke 6:12-13

Jesus demonstrated the importance of careful selection when building his team. He didn't choose based on social status, education, or wealth—factors highly valued in our society. Instead, he looked for inner qualities: ambition, values, and willingness to commit to the mission.

What struck me was how Jesus spent an entire night in prayer before selecting his twelve apostles. This deliberate approach to team building shows that transformative leaders take time to discern who should join their inner circle.

The diversity of his selections—fishermen, a tax collector, zealots—created a team with varied perspectives united by shared commitment to the mission. This diversity became a strength as they later reached different segments of society with their message.

4

Invest in Training and Development

"Then he opened their minds to understand the scriptures, and he said to them, 'Thus it is written, that the Messiah is to suffer and to rise from the dead on the third day, and that repentance and forgiveness of sins is to be proclaimed in his name to all nations.'"

— Luke 24:45-47

Jesus didn't just gather followers; he systematically trained them for their future roles. He used parables to teach complex concepts, provided hands-on experience by sending them out to practice what they had learned, and debriefed with them afterward to reinforce lessons.

Even after his resurrection, Jesus continued to teach them what they needed to know for their mission. This commitment to ongoing development prepared the disciples to face challenges they couldn't yet imagine.

As I interviewed those who had been part of Jesus's movement, I was struck by how much time he invested in preparing them. Like the best mentors I've observed in medicine, Jesus adjusted his teaching to each follower's capacity to understand and gradually increased their responsibilities.

5

Provide Correction and Guidance

"But turning and looking at his disciples, he rebuked Peter and said, 'Get behind me, Satan! For you are setting your mind not on divine things but on human things.'"

— Mark 8:33

Jesus didn't avoid difficult conversations when his followers veered off course. He corrected them firmly but constructively, addressing three problematic patterns that can undermine any team:

  • Deviation from the mission: When the disciples became distracted by their ability to drive out demons, Jesus redirected their focus to what truly mattered—their relationship with God.
  • Selfish ambition: When the disciples argued about who would be greatest, Jesus used a child as an object lesson in humility and service.
  • Betrayal and jealousy: Jesus addressed Judas's betrayal directly but without exposing him publicly before the necessary time, demonstrating wisdom in handling team conflict.

    • These corrections were never about shame or punishment; they were always aimed at growth and realignment with the mission. Jesus showed that effective leaders must address problems promptly while maintaining respect for each team member.

6

Celebrate Achievements and Recognize Potential

"Well done, good and faithful servant! You have been faithful with a few things; I will put you in charge of many things. Come and share your master's happiness!"

— Matthew 25:21

Jesus regularly affirmed his followers' growth and celebrated their successes. When Peter recognized him as the Messiah, Jesus blessed him and acknowledged the significance of his insight. When the seventy-two returned from their mission, he rejoiced with them.

Beyond recognizing past achievements, Jesus saw and named the potential in his followers. He called Simon "Peter" (the rock) long before Peter demonstrated rock-like stability. He entrusted his mother to John's care, recognizing the depth of character John would develop.

What impressed me most was how Jesus promised recognition in the future kingdom, encouraging his followers to persevere through difficulty with the assurance that their work had eternal significance. This long view of recognition sustained them through persecution and hardship.

The Lasting Impact of These Principles

As a physician who has observed many leaders in various contexts—Roman officials, synagogue leaders, and heads of households—I've seen how rare this combination of leadership qualities is. What made Jesus's approach transformative was how these six principles worked together to create a sustainable movement.

When I joined Paul on his journeys, I witnessed how these same principles were being applied in new communities across the empire. The leadership model Jesus established was adaptable to different cultures while maintaining its essential character.

Most impressively, this leadership approach worked not through coercion or imperial power but through service and love. In a world dominated by hierarchical structures, Jesus introduced a radical alternative that paradoxically proved more durable than the systems around it.
